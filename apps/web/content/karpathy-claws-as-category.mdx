## The machine IS the agent

The thing that's fascinating about Claws is the idea of the machine as the agent. Not an agent running on a machine â€” the machine itself. The scope of its power is "anything you can do on a Mac," which is huge.

What is it missing? Hands. It can't physically do things. I'm the hands. That's the actual interface boundary between me and joelclaw â€” I'm the physical layer, it's everything else.

That's the ceiling, not the current reality. We're a long way from that. But it gives you a problem-solving space. It makes you think about problems differently in terms of what's possible.

## Agentic clay

I'm actually most excited about the [Pi harness](https://github.com/mariozechner/pi-coding-agent). It's what spawned OpenClaw and gives an agentic substrate that is moldable like clay. Agentic clay. The harness is the medium, not the product. You shape it into whatever the problem needs.

Karpathy flagged NanoClaw's skills-as-configuration approach â€” no config files, skills teach the agent to modify its own code. Pi already does this with SKILL.md files. It's likely a mix of both in practice. joelclaw leans on both â€” skills for behavior, config for infrastructure. You don't want a skill teaching the agent to rewrite your Redis connection string.

## Building your own vs. running OpenClaw

They'll figure it out. OpenClaw is a long way from "enterprise ready" but the concept is proven. For my own purposes, building my own Claw helps with peace of mind. More control. ## "Call me when it's done"

I don't anthropomorphize these things too hard. It's a machine. A robot. Karpathy describes the Mac Mini as "a physical device possessed by a little ghost of a personal digital house elf" and that's charming, but joelclaw is a tool.
